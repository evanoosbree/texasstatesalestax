How Texas State and Local Sales Tax Work

Texas is a destination-based sales tax state. The state portion of the sales and use tax is 6.25% everywhere. Local jurisdictions (cities, counties, special-purpose districts and transit authorities) can each levy additional sales and use taxes, but the total local portion is capped at 2%. That means the combined rate you pay at the register can range from 6.25% up to a maximum of 8.25% depending on where the sale is sourced.

Because of this, two addresses in the same metro area can have slightly different combined rates. Merchants and remote sellers should always verify the correct rate using the state’s official Sales Tax Rate Locator before collecting or filing.

Texas Sales Tax: City-by-City Overviews

The sections below give high-level overviews for major Texas metro areas. They all share the same state rate (6.25%), but local city, county, transit and special-purpose taxes can vary. These summaries are for orientation only — they are not a substitute for an official rate lookup.

Dallas sales tax overview

Taxable sales in the Dallas area are subject to the statewide 6.25% Texas sales tax plus applicable local city, county, transit and special district taxes. Many addresses in the City of Dallas reach the maximum combined rate of 8.25%, but surrounding suburbs can differ based on which districts apply.

Houston sales tax overview

In the Houston metro, the state’s 6.25% rate applies to most taxable retail sales, and local jurisdictions may add up to 2% in additional tax. Addresses inside the city limits often reach the combined cap, while locations in surrounding counties can reflect different local combinations.

Austin sales tax overview

Austin-area sellers collect the Texas state sales tax and any local sales and use taxes that apply at the place of business or delivery. The combined rate in many Austin addresses reaches 8.25%, but rates can vary in neighboring communities and special-purpose districts.

San Antonio sales tax overview

San Antonio transactions generally include the 6.25% state portion plus city, county, transit and possibly special district tax up to the 2% local cap. Businesses that ship or deliver within the metro should verify the correct local rate for each delivery address.

Fort Worth & Tarrant County sales tax overview

Fort Worth uses the statewide 6.25% sales tax and one or more local jurisdiction rates, up to a maximum combined rate of 8.25%. Addresses in Tarrant County outside of the Fort Worth city limits can fall under different local combinations and should be checked individually.

El Paso sales tax overview

El Paso businesses collect the 6.25% state tax and local sales and use taxes that apply within city and county boundaries. Combined rates commonly approach the statewide maximum of 8.25%, but specific addresses should still be verified through the Comptroller’s tools.

Corpus Christi sales tax overview

Corpus Christi shoppers generally pay the Texas state sales tax plus applicable city, county and district taxes. As with other coastal and port areas, the combined rate can reach 8.25% depending on the local jurisdictions that apply to a given address.

Plano & North Dallas suburbs sales tax overview

Suburbs such as Plano, Frisco, McKinney and Allen layer local sales and use taxes on top of the 6.25% state rate. Many addresses reach the 8.25% cap, but overlapping special-purpose and transit districts mean that even nearby locations can differ slightly.

Lubbock sales tax overview

In Lubbock and the South Plains region, the state sales tax applies to most taxable transactions, with local city and county rates added up to the 2% local cap. Agricultural and manufacturing-related purchases may qualify for specific exemptions under Texas law.

For precise city-by-city rates, always use the official Texas Sales & Use Tax Rate Locator and follow the Comptroller’s guidance for sourcing rules and local jurisdiction boundaries.

Texas Sales Tax FAQs

Is there a separate Texas use tax?

Yes. Texas imposes a complementary use tax at the same rates as the sales tax when taxable items are purchased without paying the correct Texas tax at the time of sale (for example, items shipped in from out of state).

Are groceries and medicine taxable in Texas?

Most unprepared food for home consumption and many prescription medicines are exempt from Texas state and local sales and use tax. However, prepared food, restaurant meals and many over-the-counter items remain taxable.

Where can I find official Texas sales tax guidance?

The Texas Comptroller of Public Accounts provides official rules, publications and online tools for rate lookup, permitting and filing returns. Always rely on those resources when you have compliance questions.
